@cmam.keerthichandra6517

Bhagavatpāda Shankara assigns the adjective ’twelve’ to the term Aditya occurring in BG 10.21.

आदित्यानामहं विष्णुर्ज्योतिषां रविरंशुमान् । मरीचिर्मरुतामस्मि नक्षत्राणामहं शशी ॥ २१ ॥ 10.21

आदित्यानां द्वादशानां विष्णुः नाम आदित्यः अहम् ।

Shankara reiterates this in the Viśwarupa chapter where the term Aditya appears: 11.6

पश्यादित्यान्वसून्रुद्रानश्विनौ मरुतस्तथा । बहून्यदृष्टपूर्वाणि पश्याश्चर्याणि भारत ॥ ६ ॥ 11.6

पश्य आदित्यान् द्वादश, वसून् अष्टौ, रुद्रान् एकादश, अश्विनौ द्वौ, मरुतः सप्त सप्त गणाः ये तान् ।

For the numbers given to Aditya, Vasu, Rudra, etc. the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad lists them while excluding the Aśvini pair:

स होवाच महिमान एवैषामेते त्रयस्त्रिंशत्त्वेव देवा इति कतमे ते त्रयस्त्रिंशदित्यष्टौ वसव एकादश रुद्रा द्वादशादित्यास्त एकत्रिंशदिन्द्रश्चैव प्रजापतिश्च त्रयस्त्रिंशाविति ॥ २ ॥ 3.9.2

The Chandogya Upanishad prescribes a meditation on the Vasu, Rudra and Aditya devatas and the fruit of that upasana as the upāsaka of any of those devatas becomes one of the Eight Vasus, Eleven Rudras or Twelve Adityas as per his chosen devata:

3.6.3

स य एतदेवममृतं वेद वसूनामेवैको भूत्वाग्निनैव मुखेनैतदेवामृतं दृष्ट्वा तृप्यति स एतदेव रूपमभिसंविशत्येतस्माद्रूपादुदेति ॥ ३ ॥

  1. He who thus knows this nectar becomes one of the Vasus, with Agni (fire) at their head; he is satisfied by merely looking at the nectar. He retires into that red colour and again rises up from that colour.

3.7.3

स य एतदेवममृतं वेद रुद्राणामेवैको भूत्वेन्द्रेणैव मुखेनैतदेवामृतं दृष्ट्वा तृप्यति स एतदेव रूपमभिसंविशत्येतस्माद्रूपादुदेति ॥ ३ ॥

  1. He who thus knows this nectar becomes one of the Rudras, with Indra at their head; he is satisfied by merely looking at the nectar. He retires into that white colour and again rises up from that colour.

3.8.3

स य एतदेवममृतं वेदादित्यानामेवैको भूत्वा वरुणेनैव मुखेनैतदेवामृतं दृष्ट्वा तृप्यति स एतदेव रूपमभिसंविशत्येतस्माद्रूपादुदेति ॥ ३ ॥

  1. He who thus knows this nectar becomes one of the Adityas, with Varuna at their head; he is satisfied by merely looking at the nectar. He returns into that dark colour and again rises up from that colour.

The above mantras teach that meditators on these deities—Vasus, Rudras, and Ādityas—become those very deities. In other words, a human can attain the position of the 12 Adityas, including the one named Vishnu, through upasana. In BG 6.47 Bhāṣya too Shankara mentions योगिनामपि सर्वेषां रुद्रादित्यादिध्यानपराणां मध्ये.. among all the yogis, among those who are immersed in meditation on Rudra, Āditya, and others..

As to the names of the 12 Adityas, that being the context, there is immense information available:

Sridhari on the Vishnu Purana: https://archive.org/details/vp_vol1/page/281/mode/2up

The Bhagavata list of 12 Adityas: (Srimad Bhagavatam 12.11.27-49) https://www.harekrsna.de/surya/12adityas.htm Sridhara Swamin, in his commentaries on the Bhagavatam and the Vishnu Purana, identifies each named Aditya only as Surya/Aditya, not anything else. That’s how other schools too view the concept of the Twelve Adityas.
Sridhara Swamin states in the Bhagavatam commentary that the Kurma Purana lists the Adityas and their respective accompanying parivara devatas as 12 separate groups. एते चादित्यादयः कौमें विभज्योक्ताः ‘धाताऽर्यमा च मित्रश्च वरुणचंद्र एव च । विवस्वानथ पूषा च पर्जन्याश्रांशुरेव च । भगस्त्वष्टा च विष्णुश्च | आदित्या द्वादश स्मृताः ।

Since Shankara specified the Adityas to be 12 (based on the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad) as forms of Surya, the comparison with वृष्णीनां वासुदेवोऽस्मि (“I am Vāsudeva among the Vr̥ṣṇi clan”) BG 10.37 is completely absurd.

The appropriation of Shankara’s - ‘परमतम् अप्रतिषिद्धं अनुमतम् भवति’ is also absolutely wrong as there is only pratiṣedha. It is quite well known to those who have studied Shankara’s Prasthana traya bhashya and the introduction and the Nāma-commentary of the Vishnu Sahasra Nāma Bhashya that Shankara’s Vishnu is completely antithetical to the popular Vaishnava Vishnu.
